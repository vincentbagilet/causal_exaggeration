	\begin{center}
		\large \textsc{\textbf{Abstract}}\\
	\end{center}
		
		The credibility revolution in economics has made causal inference methods ubiquitous. At the same time, an increasing amount of evidence has highlighted that this literature strongly favors statistically significant results. I show that these two phenomena interact in a way that significantly worsens the reliability of published estimates: while causal inference methods can successfully reduce bias caused by confounders, the accompanying reduction in statistical power can create another type of bias, leading published estimates to exaggerate true effects sizes. I document this trade-off using realistic fake data simulations and a formal mathematical derivation and discuss potential avenues to address it.
